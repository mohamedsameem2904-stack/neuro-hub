Foundations of Clinical Neurology

A neurologist is a medical doctor specialized in diagnosing, treating, and managing disorders of the central and peripheral nervous systems. Unlike neurosurgeons, neurologists use non-surgical diagnostic techniques and targeted medical therapies to solve complex neurological mysteries.

The Core 6-Step Neurological Examination

1. Mental Status & Cognition Assesses orientation, memory, language, and executive function.
2. Cranial Nerves (I - XII) Tests brainstem integrity, eye movements, facial sensation, and swallowing.
3. Motor System Evaluates muscle bulk, tone, and symmetric power across major muscle groups.
4. Reflexes Grades deep tendon reflexes (e.g., patellar) and checking for pathological signs like Babinski.
5. Sensation Maps light touch, pain, temperature, vibration, and proprioception across dermatomes.
6. Coordination & Gait Tests cerebellar stability via finger-to-nose tracking, heel-to-shin movement, and natural stride.

Cerebrovascular Accident (CVA) Triage Matrix

Time is Brain: Neurological intervention mapping for acute ischemic episodes.

tPA Window: < 4.5 Hours EVT Window: < 24 Hours
Immediate Diagnostic Sequence: Stabilize ABCs → Establish Last Known Well (LKW) time → Order non-contrast emergent Head CT (essential to completely rule out intracranial hemorrhage prior to any antithrombotic therapies) → Calculate baseline NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS).
Intervention Phase Agent & Standard Dosing Mechanism of Action Critical Clinical Parameters
Hyper-Acute Thrombolysis Tenecteplase (TNKase) 0.25 mg/kg IV bolus (Max 25 mg)
Or Alteplase (tPA) 0.9 mg/kg (Max 90 mg) 		Binds to fibrin clots, converting trapped plasminogen to active plasmin, causing localized fibrinolysis. Blood Pressure must be lower than 185/110 mmHg prior to bolus. Absolute exclusion criteria: Active internal bleeding, platelet count less than 100k, recent intracranial surgery or trauma (< 3 months).
Secondary Prevention (Early) Dual Antiplatelet Therapy (DAPT) Aspirin 325 mg loading → 81 mg daily +
Clopidogrel 300–600 mg loading → 75 mg daily 		Aspirin irreversibly inhibits COX-1; Clopidogrel blocks P2Y12 ADP receptors to block downstream platelet aggregation. Indicated for minor stroke (NIHSS ≤ 3) or high-risk TIA. Initiate 24 hours *after* thrombolytic bolus, or immediately if thrombolytics were withheld. Run for 21–90 days.
Endovascular & Lipid Mgmt Atorvastatin (Lipitor) 80 mg PO daily (High-intensity) Inhibits hepatic HMG-CoA reductase; provides immediate plaque stabilization via anti-inflammatory pathways. Initiate immediately for all atherosclerotic strokes. Target objective: LDL cholesterol lower than 70 mg/dL (or a reduction greater than 50%). Monitor transaminases.

Anti-Seizure Medication (ASM) Selection Matrix

Differentiating broad-spectrum maintenance from emergent Status Epilepticus pathways.

Phase 1: Emergent (0–5 min) IV Lorazepam 4 mg or IM Midazolam 10 mg. GABA-A positive allosteric modulation to arrest acute hyper-synchronous firing.
Phase 2: Urgent (5–30 min) IV Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg or IV Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg. Re-establish seizure threshold ceiling via non-sedating load.
Phase 3: Refractory (>30 min) Continuous anesthetic infusions (Propofol, Midazolam, or Phenobarbital). Requires endotracheal intubation and ICU EEG monitoring.
Drug Class / Spectrum First-Line Indications Mechanism of Action Adverse Profiles & Monitoring
Levetiracetam (Keppra) Broad-Spectrum (Focal & Generalized) Juvenile Myoclonic Epilepsy, Generalized Tonic-Clonic, Focal onset. Binds to synaptic vesicle protein SV2A, inhibiting calcium-dependent neurotransmitter release. Red Flag: Neuropsychiatric side effects ("Keppra rage" - behavioral agitation, depression). Minimal drug-drug interactions. Cleared renally.
Lamotrigine (Lamictal) Broad-Spectrum Maintenance Idiopathic Generalized, Focal onset, highly utilized in female patients of childbearing age. Inhibits voltage-gated sodium channels, stabilizing neuronal membranes and dampening glutamate output. Black Box: Risk of Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS). Requires a strict, multi-week upward titration schedule. Dosing must be adjusted if co-administered with Valproate.
Carbamazepine (Tegretol) Narrow-Spectrum (Focal Only) Focal Seizures with/without secondary generalization. Trigeminal Neuralgia first-line. Prolongs the inactivated state of voltage-gated sodium channels. Potent CYP450 enzyme inducer (numerous drug interactions). Risk of hyponatremia (SIADH). Genetic Screen: Mandatory HLA-B*1502 screening in patients of Asian descent due to SJS risk.

Dopaminergic Titration & Modulation Matrix

Symptom management vectors for Idiopathic Parkinson's Disease (Substantia Nigra degeneration).

The Therapeutic Challenge: Long-Term Motor Fluctuations As Parkinson's advances, the therapeutic window for Levodopa narrows. Patients experience a mix of "wearing-off" effects (symptoms returning before the next dose is due) and dyskinesias (involuntary, jerky movements occurring at peak plasma concentration).
Drug Class Standard Agents Mechanism of Action Clinical Mastery Pearls & Side Effects
Gold Standard Dopamine Precursor Carbidopa / Levodopa (Sinemet) Immediate Release (IR) or Extended Release (ER)
e.g., 25/100 mg tid baseline 		Levodopa crosses the blood-brain barrier to be converted into dopamine. Carbidopa inhibits peripheral DOPA-decarboxylase, preventing peripheral breakdown and reducing severe nausea. Most effective agent for bradykinesia and rigidity. Must take 30 minutes before or 1 hour after protein-heavy meals (large neutral amino acids compete for gut absorption). Side effects include orthostatic hypotension and hallucinations.
Dopamine Receptor Agonists Pramipexole (Mirapex)
Ropinirole (Requip) 		Directly stimulates post-synaptic dopamine D2 and D3 receptors within the striatum. Often used as initial monotherapy in younger patients to delay the onset of levodopa-induced dyskinesias. Crucial Side Effect: Risk of severe Impulse Control Disorders (pathological gambling, hypersexuality, compulsive shopping). Can also cause sudden sleep attacks.
Enzymatic Breakdown Inhibitors Rasagiline (Azilect) (MAO-B) Entacapone (Comtan) (COMT) Rasagiline inhibits central MAO-B breakdown of dopamine. Entacapone blocks peripheral catechol-O-methyltransferase to extend Levodopa's half-life. Used as adjunct therapies to manage "wearing-off" fluctuations by smoothing out plasma dopamine levels. Entacapone will cause a benign, distinct orange discoloration of urine. Watch for serotonin syndrome risk with MAO-B blockers.
